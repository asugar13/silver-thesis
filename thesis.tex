\begin{abstract}
\noindent
% DRAFT abstract — qualitative, to be finalised once all results tables are frozen.
This thesis asks how much of silver's short-term price behaviour can be forecast, and
frames the question through the efficient-market hypothesis (EMH). Silver is modelled at
the weekly (Friday-to-Friday) horizon over 2015--2026, and the analysis is factorised
into the two moments: the conditional \emph{mean} of returns and the conditional
\emph{variance}---efficiency constrains the former but not the latter. For the mean, a
deliberate ladder of models of increasing flexibility---ARIMA/ARIMAX, VAR,
mixed-data-sampling (MIDAS) regressions, random forests, gradient boosting, and an
LSTM---is tested against a common random-walk-with-drift benchmark, expanding the
information set from silver's own past (weak-form EMH) to a broad set of public
information (semi-strong-form EMH): cross-asset returns, silver futures positioning,
macroeconomic releases, technical indicators, and sentiment from Reddit and financial
news. For the variance, weekly realised volatility is modelled with GARCH and the
heterogeneous autoregressive (HAR) model, and the question of interest is whether
sentiment improves the forecast. The central finding is a single coherent statement: no
model beats the drift benchmark for the conditional mean by a significant
margin, so weak- and semi-strong-form efficiency cannot be rejected---yet the
conditional variance is strongly and significantly forecastable, with sentiment adding a
small but robust increment. Predictable volatility alongside an unpredictable mean is
exactly what the EMH permits, so the two results are complementary rather than
contradictory.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Silver lives a double life. It is an industrial input---roughly half of annual demand
goes into photovoltaics, electronics, brazing alloys, and medical uses, tying its price
to the global manufacturing cycle---and, at the same time, a monetary metal held as a
store of value, an inflation hedge, and a more volatile cousin of gold. On top of both
sits a retail following that can sometimes turn silver into a meme trade: in late January
2021, Reddit's r/WallStreetSilver drove spot from roughly \$25 to \$30 an ounce within
days before it faded. This blend of industrial demand, monetary hedging, and episodic
retail attention makes silver an unusually rich laboratory for the oldest question in
empirical finance: how much of a price can be forecast?

That question has a canonical answer, and it is mostly negative. The efficient-market
hypothesis \parencite{fama1970,fama1991} holds that prices already embed available
information, so successive price changes are approximately unpredictable. It is conventionally stated in three nested forms, graded by the
information set a forecaster is allowed to use:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Weak form} --- past prices and returns carry no exploitable signal.
    \item \textbf{Semi-strong form} --- no \emph{public} information does either: not
    prices, fundamentals, news, or macroeconomic data.
    \item \textbf{Strong form} --- not even \emph{private}, inside information does.
\end{itemize}

\noindent Each form nests the one before it, so the claims grow progressively stronger.
For a liquid, globally traded metal, weak- and semi-strong-form efficiency are the
natural hypotheses to take to the data, and they are the two this thesis tests.

Empirically, silver's \emph{price} behaves like a random walk with drift: it is
non-stationary, and shocks accumulate rather than mean-revert
(Figure~\ref{fig:silver-price}). Returns, however, are stationary---they fluctuate around
a stable long-run mean rather than wandering without bound. The object worth modelling is
therefore not the price level but its increment, the weekly log-return
\(r_t = \log P_t - \log P_{t-1}\); stationarity is what makes the forecasting problem
well-posed, giving a fixed quantity to predict and a natural yardstick---that same
prevailing average---to judge any forecast against.

The weekly (Friday-to-Friday) horizon is a deliberate choice of frequency, made where the
information aligns. Futures-market positioning is reported weekly and macroeconomic
releases monthly, while daily sentiment is too sparse and noisy to inform a forecast and
daily returns are dominated by microstructure noise; a weekly horizon is coarse enough to
average out that noise yet fine enough to leave roughly 600 observations over
2015--2026---enough to train and properly out-of-sample-test the machine-learning models.
Weekly realised volatility, the target of the second-moment analysis, is in turn
aggregated from daily returns.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/output.png}
    \caption{Silver front-month futures (SI=F), daily, January 2015--May 2026. The
    annotated episodes punctuate the sample: the COVID-19 crash (March 2020), the
    r/WallStreetSilver squeeze (January 2021), the start of the Federal Reserve's hiking
    cycle (March 2022), and the 2024--2026 bull run that carried spot to an all-time high
    near \$115/oz before a sharp ($-41\%$) correction. The level is non-stationary and
    shocks accumulate; the thesis models its stationary weekly log-return rather than the
    level itself.}
    \label{fig:silver-price}
\end{figure} The efficiency question then becomes whether \(r_t\)
can be forecast---and silver's own past offers little to go on. Weekly
returns carry almost no linear autocorrelation, so a linear own-history model such as
ARIMA has little to grip; a formal model search duly collapses it to a constant mean. Yet
the returns are not independent noise either, because their \emph{magnitude} is
persistent---volatile weeks cluster together---so structure remains, only in the variance
rather than the mean. The thesis follows this thread: it asks first whether silver's own
history forecasts the return, then whether the broad public-information set a real
forecaster would possess does---cross-asset returns (gold, the US dollar, copper,
equities, oil, and the VIX), futures-market positioning, macroeconomic releases,
technical indicators, and retail and news sentiment---and, separately, whether the
predictable variance can be modelled.

\textbf{Research questions.} The thesis is built around four questions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Q1 (weak form).} Are weekly silver returns forecastable from their
    own past?
    \item \textbf{Q2 (semi-strong form).} Does expanding the information set to public
    covariates help---and does it matter whether the relationship is assumed linear or
    allowed to be nonlinear?
    \item \textbf{Q3 (the second moment).} Is weekly realised volatility forecastable,
    and if so by how much?
    \item \textbf{Q4 (sentiment).} Does retail and news sentiment add incremental
    predictive content to either the conditional mean or the conditional variance?
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Approach.} To answer these questions, the analysis estimates a deliberate ladder
of models of increasing flexibility against a common benchmark and a unified evaluation
protocol. The benchmark
throughout is the prevailing-mean ``drift'' forecast---the random-walk-with-drift,
equivalent to ARIMA(0,0,0)---which is the correct null for a return target
\parencite{welch2008,campbell2008}. For the conditional mean, the ladder runs from
classical time-series models (ARIMA/ARIMAX, VAR, and MIDAS regressions) through tree
ensembles (random forests and gradient boosting) to a recurrent neural network (LSTM);
for the conditional variance it runs GARCH and the heterogeneous autoregressive (HAR)
model of \textcite{corsi2009} alongside the same tree ensembles.

\section{Conceptual Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

% PLACEMENT NOTE (for the author): this section is the home of the random-walk /
% martingale-difference / information-set notation. The intro states the efficiency
% question in plain English; this section gives it the precise form that motivates the
% model ladder; the Conclusion (sec:conclusion) revisits the one-paragraph synthesis.
% Drafted concisely below; expand per notebooks/weekly/formulas.md as needed.

This section fixes the notation used throughout and states, precisely, the null hypothesis that
each model class is built to test. The aim is to keep the language exact without
overclaiming: the empirical results are framed as tests of the conditional mean and the
conditional variance of weekly silver returns, not as claims about the full return
distribution.

\subsection{Prices, returns, and the random walk}
\label{sec:framework-rw}

Let \(P_t\) be the silver price and \(p_t = \log P_t\) the log price. The weekly
log-return is the increment in the log price,
\begin{equation}
    r_{t+1} = p_{t+1} - p_t, \qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad p_{t+1} = p_t + r_{t+1},
\end{equation}
so the price level and the return are two views of the same process: the level
accumulates shocks, while returns \emph{are} the shocks. A random walk with drift writes
the level as \(p_{t+1} = p_t + \mu + \varepsilon_{t+1}\) with
\(\E[\varepsilon_{t+1}] = 0\). Silver log prices are best described as
\emph{random-walk-like}---non-stationary, with shocks that accumulate---which is the
reason the modelling target throughout is the stationary increment \(r_t\) rather than
the level. The safer wording avoids the \emph{strict} random walk: the innovations are
not independent and identically distributed, because their variance clusters
(Section~\ref{sec:framework-var}).

\subsection{The conditional mean and the information set}
\label{sec:framework-mds}

Let \(F_t\) denote the information available at time \(t\). For the weak-form test, this
is silver's own past, \(F_t = \{r_t, r_{t-1}, r_{t-2}, \dots\}\). Returns satisfy the
\emph{martingale difference} property with respect to \(F_t\) if their conditional mean is
constant,
\begin{equation}
    \E[r_{t+1} \mid F_t] = \mu,
    \label{eq:weak-null}
\end{equation}
or, equivalently, the demeaned return \(r_{t+1} - \mu\) is a \emph{martingale difference
sequence} (MDS),
\begin{equation}
    \E[r_{t+1} - \mu \mid F_t] = 0.
    \label{eq:weak-null-mds}
\end{equation}
Past returns do not move the expected next-week return away from its long-run average.
This is a statement about the first moment \emph{only}; it does not assert that returns
are i.i.d. The semi-strong test expands the information set to public covariates,
\begin{equation}
    G_t = \{F_t,\ \text{gold},\ \text{USD},\ \text{copper},\ \text{VIX},\ \text{oil},\
    \text{COT},\ \text{macro},\ \text{technicals},\ \text{sentiment}\},
\end{equation}
and asks whether the martingale-difference property survives the larger conditioning set,
\begin{equation}
    \E[r_{t+1} \mid G_t] = \mu.
    \label{eq:ss-null}
\end{equation}
ARIMA tests whether silver returns are a martingale difference with respect to their own
past, Equation~\eqref{eq:weak-null}; ARIMAX and the other models test whether
that property survives once the information set is expanded to public covariates,
Equation~\eqref{eq:ss-null}. Operationally, these nulls are tested as ``does the model
beat the prevailing-mean (drift) benchmark out of sample?''---a forecast that beats the
drift would contradict the corresponding null.

\subsection{Linear and nonlinear alternatives}
\label{sec:framework-nonlin}

The alternative to Equation~\eqref{eq:ss-null} can be specified narrowly or broadly. A
linear model entertains an additive alternative,
\begin{equation}
    \E[r_{t+1} \mid G_t] = \mu + \beta' G_t,
\end{equation}
so ARIMAX, VAR, and MIDAS ask whether public covariates enter the conditional mean
linearly and additively. Nonlinear models entertain the broader alternative
\begin{equation}
    \E[r_{t+1} \mid G_t] = f(G_t),
\end{equation}
in which the conditional mean can respond to thresholds and interactions among the
covariates rather than to each one additively---a signal might be invisible in any single
predictor yet surface in a combination of them. Random forests, gradient boosting, and the
LSTM search this larger space.
The logic of the ladder is therefore cumulative: if even flexible nonlinear models fail
to beat the drift benchmark out of sample, the martingale-difference interpretation is
\emph{strengthened} rather than merely assumed from linear tests.

\subsection{The conditional variance}
\label{sec:framework-var}

Efficiency constrains Equation~\eqref{eq:ss-null}; it places no restriction on the
conditional variance \(\operatorname{Var}(r_{t+1}\mid F_t)\). The two can diverge: the
mean may be (approximately) constant while the variance moves through time, which is
exactly what volatility clustering means---a large move today says little about the sign
or size of next week's return but does signal that next week is likely to be more
volatile. Strict white noise would require independence in \(r_t\), \(r_t^2\), and
\(|r_t|\) alike; the MDS property in Equation~\eqref{eq:weak-null} restricts only the
first of these. Weekly silver returns are close to white noise in the mean but not
strict white noise, because squared returns are strongly autocorrelated. The predictable
structure is therefore concentrated in the second moment, and the squared return is its
natural proxy. By definition it splits into the variance and the squared mean, and at the
weekly horizon the latter is negligible:
\begin{equation}
    \E[r_t^2]
    = \underbrace{\operatorname{Var}(r_t)}_{\approx\,1.9\times10^{-3}}
    + \underbrace{\big(\E[r_t]\big)^2}_{\approx\,7\times10^{-6}}
    \;\approx\; \operatorname{Var}(r_t).
    \label{eq:var-proxy}
\end{equation}
The squared mean is only \(0.4\%\) of the variance, so a squared return is---to that
approximation---a model-free measure of variance. This is what the volatility chapter
(Section~\ref{sec:var}) builds on, summing daily squared returns into weekly realised
volatility.
Figure~\ref{fig:returns-acf} shows both moments at once for the weekly silver return.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/returns_acf.png}
    \caption{The two moments of the weekly silver log-return, 2015--2026. \emph{Left:}
    the return series---stationary, fluctuating around a small positive drift
    (mean \(\approx 0.27\%\) per week). \emph{Centre:} the sample autocorrelation of returns
    lies almost entirely within the 95\% white-noise band (dashed), with only isolated
    lags breaching it---consistent with the martingale-difference null of
    Equation~\eqref{eq:weak-null}, so a linear own-history model has little to exploit.
    \emph{Right:} the autocorrelation of \emph{squared} returns is large and positive
    across the first several lags---the volatility clustering of
    Section~\ref{sec:framework-var}. The mean is close to unpredictable; the variance is
    not.}
    \label{fig:returns-acf}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Direction versus magnitude}
\label{sec:framework-direction}

A final distinction governs how forecasts are scored. Directional accuracy asks whether
\(\operatorname{sign}(\hat r_{t+1}) = \operatorname{sign}(r_{t+1})\) more often than
chance, which is \emph{not} the same as beating the drift benchmark under squared-error
loss: a model can call the sign correctly yet fail to improve out-of-sample \(R^2\).
Because efficiency is a conditional-mean claim, the magnitude axis (RMSE, out-of-sample
\(R^2\), and Diebold--Mariano against the drift) is load-bearing, while directional
accuracy---and its significance test, Pesaran--Timmermann \parencite{pesaran1992}---is a
secondary, economically motivated lens. Directional gains are interesting but do not by
themselves reject the martingale-difference null.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:lit}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This thesis sits at the intersection of market efficiency, commodity forecasting,
volatility modelling, and text-based investor attention. Its central distinction is between
two kinds of predictability. The first is \emph{conditional-mean} predictability: whether
next week's silver return can be forecast from past prices or public information. This is
the economically load-bearing question for the efficient-market hypothesis. The second is
\emph{conditional-variance} predictability: whether the size of next week's move can be
forecast, even if its sign and expected value cannot. The literature suggests that these two
forms of predictability should not be treated as contradictory. A market can be efficient in
the mean while still displaying persistent volatility, because the EMH restricts expected
returns, not time-varying risk.

\subsection{Return predictability, market efficiency, and public information}

The natural starting point is the martingale logic of \textcite{samuelson1965} and the
efficient-market taxonomy of \textcite{fama1970,fama1991}. In an informationally efficient
market, prices incorporate available information quickly enough that price changes are
difficult to forecast from that same information. The weak form asks whether past prices and
returns contain exploitable signal; the semi-strong form asks whether a broader public
information set does. That the weak-form null can be rejected is itself well
established---\textcite{lomackinlay1988}, with a variance-ratio test on \emph{weekly}
returns, reject the random walk for US equity indices---so own-history predictability at
the weekly horizon is possible in principle, and testing for it in silver is a genuine
question rather than a foregone conclusion. For this thesis, the implication is direct: an
ARIMA model tests
whether silver's own return history moves the conditional mean, while ARIMAX, VAR, MIDAS,
machine-learning models, and the LSTM test whether public covariates do. If none improves
materially on the prevailing-mean drift benchmark out of sample, the result is evidence in
favour of weak- and semi-strong-form efficiency at the weekly horizon.

Commodity markets complicate this simple null, because prices reflect both financial and
physical forces. \textcite{deaton1992} document the extreme persistence and spike-like
behaviour of commodity prices, which makes price levels poor forecasting targets and
motivates modelling returns instead. At the same time, commodity futures returns have been
linked to hedging pressure, inventories, basis, and risk premia
\parencite{bessembinder1992,gorton2006,gorton2013,szymanowska2014}. These papers justify
including futures positioning and cross-asset information, but they do not imply that a
single weekly silver return should be easy to forecast out of sample. The distinction
matters: variables may explain risk premia or cross-sectional commodity returns over longer
horizons without generating a reliable one-week forecast for silver's conditional mean.

Silver is also not simply ``gold with more volatility.'' Precious-metals research shows that
gold, silver, platinum, and palladium respond differently to macroeconomic conditions.
\textcite{batten2010}, for example, find that the macroeconomic drivers of volatility differ
across precious metals, while \textcite{zhang2010} document important linkages between gold
and oil. This supports the thesis's broad public-information set---gold, the dollar, copper,
oil, equities, the VIX, rates, inflation expectations, industrial production, and COT
positioning---while also warning against assuming that silver's behaviour can be inferred
from gold alone. The CFTC's Commitments of Traders data are especially relevant because they
separate commercial and non-commercial positioning, making them a natural proxy for hedging
pressure and speculative sentiment.

The benchmarking literature is equally important. \textcite{welch2008} show that many
variables that appear to predict returns in sample fail to beat the historical average out
of sample. \textcite{campbell2008} argue that small gains are possible when forecasts are
disciplined by theory, but they retain the historical mean as the central benchmark and
out-of-sample \(R^2\) as the natural effect-size measure. This thesis follows that
discipline. The drift forecast is not a weak straw man; it is the operational version of the
EMH null for a return target.

The adaptive-markets hypothesis of \textcite{lo2004} provides a useful interpretation for
unstable or episodic findings. Under the AMH, market efficiency is not a fixed state but an
evolutionary outcome shaped by competition, learning, institutional change, and changing
trader populations. A temporary directional signal around a retail-attention episode, a
crisis, or a commodity squeeze would therefore not be surprising. What matters is whether it
survives out of sample, across regimes, and against a benchmark. This is why the thesis
combines expanding and rolling windows, subperiod robustness checks, and a separate
interpretation of directional accuracy.

\subsection{Mixed-frequency information and nonlinear forecasting models}

The semi-strong test raises a practical problem: the information set is observed at different
frequencies. Silver and cross-asset prices are daily, COT positioning is weekly,
macroeconomic variables are monthly and released with delays, and sentiment can be daily but
sparse. MIDAS regression was developed precisely for this kind of problem.
\textcite{ghysels2004,ghysels2007} show how high-frequency lags can enter a low-frequency
forecast through a parsimonious lag-weight function rather than a large unrestricted lag
set. \textcite{andreou2013} demonstrate the value of daily financial information for
macroeconomic forecasting. Here, the logic is inverted: monthly macro releases and daily
financial and sentiment variables are aligned to a weekly silver-return target. MIDAS is
therefore not an exotic addition; it is the appropriate linear bridge between
mixed-frequency public information and the weekly EMH test.

Machine-learning models extend the alternative hypothesis from linear to nonlinear
predictability. Random forests and gradient boosting can detect thresholds and interactions;
LSTMs can learn sequential patterns without hand-specifying every lag. This is important
because a failure of ARIMAX or VAR alone would only reject simple linear predictability, not
nonlinear predictability. The deeper forecasting literature, however, urges caution.
\textcite{sezer2020} review a large body of deep-learning studies in financial time series
and find many reported gains, but the broader forecasting literature shows that more complex
methods do not automatically beat simpler benchmarks, and that rankings often depend on the
evaluation metric and test design. The thesis therefore uses machine learning not as a
promise of superior performance, but as a stricter test of efficiency: if flexible nonlinear
models also fail to beat the drift, the mean-efficiency conclusion becomes stronger.

\subsection{Directional accuracy versus magnitude accuracy}

A separate strand of the literature evaluates whether forecasts get the \emph{direction} of
returns right, rather than their magnitude. \textcite{pesaran1992} provide a formal test for
market-timing ability, and directional metrics remain popular in economics and finance
because traders often care about whether prices rise or fall. But directional accuracy is
not the same object as RMSE, MAE, or out-of-sample \(R^2\). A model can call slightly more
signs correctly while still making large errors on the weeks that matter most; conversely, a
model can reduce squared error without producing a useful trading rule. \textcite{leitch1991}
make the broader point that conventional statistical error measures and economic
profitability can rank forecasts differently. \textcite{christoffersen2006} similarly treat
direction-of-change forecasting as a distinct problem from point forecasting.

For an EMH thesis, this distinction is crucial. The weak- and semi-strong-form hypotheses
are fundamentally about whether the conditional expected return moves away from its benchmark
value. Therefore RMSE, out-of-sample \(R^2\), and the Diebold--Mariano test against the
drift are the primary evidence. Directional accuracy is still informative, especially under
the adaptive-markets interpretation: a transient sign signal may reveal episodic market
structure, behavioural pressure, or regime dependence. But unless that signal improves
magnitude forecasts, survives robustness checks, and is economically exploitable after
costs, it should be described as directional dependence, not as a rejection of mean
efficiency.

\subsection{Variance predictability: GARCH, realised volatility, and HAR}

The volatility literature begins from the opposite empirical fact: although returns are hard
to forecast, their magnitudes are persistent. The ARCH model of \textcite{engle1982} and the
GARCH extension of \textcite{bollerslev1986} formalised volatility clustering by allowing
conditional variance to depend on past shocks and past variance. This is now one of the most
established facts in financial econometrics. For this thesis, the implication is that
predictable volatility does not weaken the EMH result. It strengthens the mean-variance
interpretation: silver may be efficient in expected return while still risky in a
forecastable way.

Realised-volatility models sharpen the target by replacing latent volatility with a
model-free proxy built from higher-frequency squared returns. \textcite{barndorffnielsen2002}
and \textcite{andersen2003} helped establish realised variance as a practical object for
volatility measurement and forecasting. The HAR model of \textcite{corsi2009} then became a
benchmark because it captures long-memory-like volatility persistence using simple averages
over short, medium, and long horizons. \textcite{patton2011} adds an important evaluation
point: because realised volatility is itself a noisy proxy for latent variance, forecast
comparisons should use losses that are robust to measurement error, especially QLIKE. This
justifies the thesis's use of GARCH and HAR as volatility benchmarks and QLIKE-based
Diebold--Mariano tests as the main variance-comparison evidence.

\subsection{Sentiment, attention, and social media}

The sentiment literature gives the thesis its most novel extension. \textcite{tetlock2007}
shows that media pessimism contains information for stock prices, while \textcite{baker2006}
and \textcite{barber2008} connect investor sentiment and attention to investor behaviour.
\textcite{da2011} introduce Google search volume as a direct measure of retail attention,
and \textcite{antweiler2004} show that internet message-board activity contains more reliable
information about trading activity and volatility than about returns. This distinction
matches the thesis's expected result: sentiment and attention may matter more for the
variance of silver returns than for the mean.

Modern NLP improves measurement. FinBERT is designed for financial language and outperforms
generic methods on financial sentiment tasks \parencite{araci2019}, while RoBERTa and
Twitter-specialised models improve contextual understanding for informal, rapidly changing
online language \parencite{liu2019,loureiro2022}. This motivates using FinBERT for financial
news and Twitter-RoBERTa-style models for Reddit rather than a single generic dictionary.
The measurement choice is therefore grounded in the literature: domain matters, and Reddit
language is not the same as financial-news language.

The evidence on Reddit-style attention is mixed but highly relevant. Studies of WallStreetBets
and GameStop \parencite{lyocsa2022} show that Reddit activity can precede or accompany extreme
retail-driven market episodes, but newer evidence often finds that raw activity, comment volume,
and Google Trends are stronger signals than sentiment polarity alone. A focused literature search did not
surface a mature, peer-reviewed silver-only equivalent; the closest evidence comes from
broader equity, Reddit, and volatility studies. That absence is useful: it creates the gap
this thesis fills by testing Reddit and news sentiment directly in silver, separating tone
from attention, and asking whether either adds incremental value to mean or variance
forecasts.

Taken together, the literature supports the thesis design. The conditional mean is the
correct object for weak- and semi-strong-form efficiency tests; the drift benchmark is the
correct null; nonlinear models are useful because they make the test harder to pass;
directional accuracy is interesting but secondary; and volatility is expected to be more
forecastable than returns. Sentiment is therefore best treated not as a guaranteed return
predictor, but as an empirical ablation that may capture attention-driven volatility,
especially in a market like silver where industrial demand, monetary hedging, and retail
narratives can collide.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

All models are estimated on one weekly dataset, January 2015 to May 2026, on a common
Friday-to-Friday (W-FRI) calendar of 591 weeks. Seven data source families are consolidated
into a single feature frame (Section~\ref{sec:data-frame}); the forecast target throughout
is the weekly silver log-return \(r_t = \log P_t - \log P_{t-1}\). The information set spans
seven source families, all observed at the Friday-\(t{-}1\) close and lagged before use
(Section~\ref{sec:data-frame}):

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Market prices} (Yahoo Finance, daily) --- silver, the forecast target
    (COMEX front-month \texttt{SI=F}), and the six cross-asset benchmarks gold, copper, the
    US dollar index, the S\&P~500, the VIX, and oil, entered as weekly log-returns (the EXOG
    set) \parencite{batten2010,zhang2010}.
    \item \textbf{Rates and inflation} (FRED, daily/weekly) --- the 10-year TIPS real yield,
    10-year breakeven inflation, and initial jobless claims, each as a weekly change.
    \item \textbf{Macroeconomic} (FRED, monthly) --- CPI, the federal funds rate, industrial
    production, and M2, entered under a point-in-time publication-availability rule.
    \item \textbf{Positioning} (CFTC Commitments of Traders, weekly) --- managed-money and
    commercial net positioning on COMEX silver, as a share of open interest.
    \item \textbf{Sentiment} (Reddit, GDELT, NewsAPI.ai, Google Trends) --- retail and news
    tone and attention, scored with Twitter-RoBERTa for Reddit \parencite{loureiro2022} and
    FinBERT for news \parencite{araci2019}, averaged to a weekly mean.
    \item \textbf{Business sentiment (PMI)} (FRED, monthly) --- a China business-confidence
    proxy for industrial demand, entered as a point-in-time level.
\end{itemize}

The two text-news feeds differ in coverage: GDELT is sparse---it begins only in late 2017
and has zero-article weeks even inside the test window, a documented limitation---while the
paid NewsAPI.ai feed spans the full sample and is the cleaner news source; the volatility
chapter (Section~\ref{sec:var}) draws its attention and sentiment signals from Reddit, paid
news, and Google Trends instead.
Appendix~\ref{app:data} catalogues every variable (Table~\ref{tab:variables}) and details
each individual series.

\subsection{Technical indicators}

The TECH ablation group collects momentum and trend transforms of silver's own weekly

\emph{variance} rather than the conditional mean a return forecast targets, so they carry

\subsection{Sentiment and attention features}
\label{sec:data-sentiment}

The volatility chapter (Section~\ref{sec:method-var}) reads each text source through two
derived signals---how \emph{much} is written (attention) and how \emph{strong} the tone is
(intensity)---rather than the signed weekly tone the return models use. For a week \(t\) with
\(n_t\) items (Reddit posts or news articles) and weekly-mean tone \(\bar s_t \in [-1,1]\),
\begin{equation}
    \text{attention}_t = \log\!\left(1 + n_t\right), \qquad
    \text{intensity}_t = \lvert \bar s_t \rvert .
    \label{eq:attn-int}
\end{equation}
Attention is logged because weekly volume is heavily right-skewed (Reddit: median \(\approx
330\), max \(\approx 9{,}000\) posts); intensity drops the sign because for a volatility
target it is the \emph{magnitude} of sentiment, not its direction, that should move the
second moment. Both are formed for Reddit (Twitter-RoBERTa tone) and the paid-news feed
(FinBERT title tone) and lagged one week. Google Trends adds a third, broad-retail attention
proxy: the log of Google's search-volume index (SVI) for the query ``silver''---a weekly
series Google scales to \(0\)--\(100\) over the sample \parencite{da2011}---so
\(\text{trends}_t = \log(\mathrm{SVI}_t)\), with full 2015--2026 coverage where the news
feeds are sparse.

\subsection{Feature frame and split}
\label{sec:data-frame}

Daily series are aggregated to a common weekly (W-FRI) grid by fixed rules: returns and
FRED changes are summed over the week, sentiment is averaged, and levels (e.g.\
\texttt{gs\_ratio\_z}) and COT positioning take the week's last value. Every feature is then
lagged before it enters a model, so a value at the Friday-\(t{-}1\) close forecasts the
Friday-\(t\) return with no intra-week look-ahead---most by a uniform one week, the monthly
macro block at its actual publication lag, and a best-lag ablation set at a pre-selected lag
(for each predictor, the one most correlated with the target in-sample).

The split is chronological, cut on Friday boundaries: 2015--2021 for training, 2022 for
validation, and 2023 to May 2026 as the held-out test window (Table~\ref{tab:split}). This

genuine regime shift rather than on an in-sample echo of it.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Forecasting targets and benchmarks}
\label{sec:method-target}

The target for the conditional-mean analysis is the weekly silver log-return \(r_t\) of
Section~\ref{sec:framework-rw}, forecast one week ahead. Two benchmarks frame every model.
The first is a \emph{na\"ive} forecast, \(\hat r_{t+1} = r_t\), the persistence of last
week's return; it is a weak reference for a near-white-noise target and is reported only
for context. The load-bearing benchmark is the \emph{drift}, the prevailing (expanding)
mean of past returns,
\begin{equation}
    \hat r_{t+1}^{\,\text{drift}} = \frac{1}{t}\sum_{s=1}^{t} r_s,
    \label{eq:drift}
\end{equation}
which is the random-walk-with-drift forecast and is identical to \(\mathrm{ARIMA}(0,0,0)\)
on an expanding window. It is the correct null for a return target
\parencite{welch2008,campbell2008}: a model that cannot beat the drift out of sample
carries no exploitable conditional-mean information, so ``beating the drift'' is the
operational form of rejecting the efficiency null hypotheses of Equations~\eqref{eq:weak-null}
and~\eqref{eq:ss-null}.

The second-moment analysis (Section~\ref{sec:var}) swaps the return for weekly
\emph{realised volatility}: realised variance is additive across days, so the weekly figure
is the square root of the summed daily squared returns,
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{RV}_t = \sqrt{\textstyle\sum_{i \in \text{week } t} r_i^2 },
    \label{eq:rv}
\end{equation}
a model-free volatility proxy. Here the load-bearing floor is the \emph{na\"ive} random-walk
forecast \(\widehat{\mathrm{RV}}_{t+1} = \mathrm{RV}_t\)---last week's volatility carried
forward, which has zero direction-of-change accuracy by construction---the second-moment
counterpart of the drift null for the mean.

Models are evaluated by \emph{walk-forward} one-step-ahead forecasting on the held-out
2023--2026 window (Section~\ref{sec:data-frame}): at each week a model is fit on data
through \(t\) and predicts \(t+1\), so no future information enters a forecast. Where the
model permits, two windowing schemes are compared---an \emph{expanding} window that grows
with the sample, and a \emph{rolling} 100-week window that discards older data to track
regime change. Re-estimation cadence is model-specific and noted below.

\subsection{Conditional-mean models}
\label{sec:method-mean}

The models form a deliberate ladder of increasing flexibility---from linear own-history,
through linear public-information, to nonlinear machine learning---every rung judged
against the same drift floor. Each exogenous predictor enters lagged one week
(Section~\ref{sec:data-frame}), and the ablation \emph{ladder} adds feature groups
cumulatively to a six-asset cross-return base (EXOG): the gold/silver ratio, technical
indicators, monthly macro, the China PMI proxy, daily FRED indicators, COT positioning, and
Reddit, GDELT, and reputable paid-news sentiment, up to an all-features rung.

\textbf{ARIMA / ARIMAX.} An \(\mathrm{ARIMA}(p,d,q)\) model expresses the \(d\)-th difference
of the return through its own past values and past innovations,
\begin{equation}
    \phi(L)\,(1-L)^d r_t = \mu + \theta(L)\,\varepsilon_t, \qquad
    \phi(L) = 1 - \sum_{i=1}^{p}\phi_i L^i, \quad
    \theta(L) = 1 + \sum_{j=1}^{q}\theta_j L^j ,
\end{equation}
where \(L\) is the lag operator and \(\varepsilon_t\) is white noise. The order \((p,d,q)\) is
chosen by AIC on the training and validation data; the search selects \(\mathrm{ARIMA}(0,0,0)\)
---that is, \(r_t = \mu + \varepsilon_t\)---so the best own-history model coincides with the
drift and gives the cleanest weak-form test. ARIMAX adds the lagged exogenous groups as
regressors,
\begin{equation}
    r_t = \mu + \beta' x_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t ,
\end{equation}
where \(x_{t-1}\) collects the lagged exogenous groups added cumulatively along the ablation
ladder above (the gold/silver ratio, technical indicators, FRED daily and monthly macro, the
PMI proxy, COT positioning and the sentiment feeds); this supplies the linear semi-strong test.
As a complementary series-level weak-form diagnostic, the generalised
spectral test of \textcite{hong2003} checks for nonlinear own-history departures from
the martingale-difference null that linear autocorrelation diagnostics may miss.

\textbf{VAR.} A vector autoregression of silver and the cross-asset returns, stacked in a
vector \(y_t\), with lag order \(p\) chosen by AIC,
\begin{equation}
    y_t = c + \sum_{i=1}^{p} A_i\, y_{t-i} + \varepsilon_t ,
\end{equation}
which captures linear feedback among the assets; Granger-causality and impulse-response
diagnostics accompany it descriptively in the project repository.

\textbf{MIDAS.} Mixed-data-sampling regressions keep a predictor's recent high-frequency
path instead of collapsing it to a single weekly number, and learn how much each lag
should matter. A high-frequency predictor's \(K\) lags are compressed into one term through
a normalised lag-weight curve,
\begin{equation}
    m_{j,t}(\theta_j) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_j(k;\theta_j)\, x_{j,t-k},
    \qquad \sum_{k=1}^{K} w_j(k;\theta_j) = 1,
\end{equation}
where the weights follow the Beta or exponential-Almon family
\parencite{ghysels2004,ghysels2007}. The weekly forecast then regresses the silver return
on its own autoregressive lags and the compressed mixed-frequency blocks,
\begin{equation}
    r_t = \alpha + \sum_{i=1}^{p}\phi_i\, r_{t-i}
    + \sum_{j} \beta_j\, m_{j,t}^{(d)}(\theta_j)
    + \sum_{m} \delta_m\, m_{m,t}^{(M)}(\eta_m)
    + \sum_{q} \gamma_q\, q_{q,t-1}
    + \varepsilon_t ,
\end{equation}
where \(m^{(d)}\) are daily MIDAS blocks, \(m^{(M)}\) publication-lagged monthly-macro
blocks, and \(q_{q,t-1}\) weekly-release controls (jobless claims, COT). The shape
parameters are estimated by nonlinear least squares (the linear coefficients profiled
out), with the weight family selected on the validation window. The payoff is parsimony:
rather than \(K=20\) free coefficients per daily predictor, two weight parameters trace a
smooth lag profile, which is what makes daily information usable in a weekly forecast
without overfitting. Two variants are run---monthly macro at three lags, and a
multi-frequency model adding the six cross-asset returns at 20 daily lags
\parencite{andreou2013}.

\textbf{Random forest and gradient boosting.} Nonlinear ensembles that admit thresholds
and interactions among the lagged features---a random forest \parencite{breiman2001} and
gradient-boosted trees, XGBoost \parencite{chen2016}. Hyperparameters are grid-searched per
ablation rung on the training-plus-validation data using time-series cross-validation---contiguous
expanding folds that always validate on weeks \emph{after} those used to fit, so no future
information leaks into tuning---and the model is re-fit every four weeks in the walk-forward.

\textbf{LSTM.} A long short-term memory recurrent network \parencite{hochreiter1997} that
reads a fixed window of recent weeks and learns its own representation of the past; a
single recurrent layer with dropout and early stopping is used, given the small sample (a
few hundred training sequences). A weekly-input model and a daily-input variant (a
60-trading-day window) are estimated, the latter testing whether daily-resolution
information helps forecast the weekly return.

The classical and tree models are run under both windowing schemes; the LSTM uses a single
expanding-window training pass, since a 100-week rolling window leaves too few sequences to
train stably. In all, the conditional-mean analysis spans six model families---ARIMA/ARIMAX,
VAR, MIDAS, the random forest, XGBoost, and the LSTM---plus two daily-input variants (for MIDAS and LSTM) that read the higher-frequency data directly, for eight specifications judged
against the common drift floor.

\subsection{Conditional-variance models}
\label{sec:method-var}

The second-moment analysis models weekly realised volatility (\(\mathrm{RV}_t\),
Eq.~\ref{eq:rv} in Section~\ref{sec:method-target}). Its predictors are the heterogeneous-autoregressive (HAR)
lags---1-week, monthly (4-week mean), and quarterly (12-week mean) trailing realised
volatility---the three horizons that, in Corsi's heterogeneous-market account
\parencite{corsi2009}, proxy short-, medium-, and long-horizon traders and together reproduce
volatility's long-memory persistence parsimoniously. They are
optionally extended with the six cross-asset realised volatilities,
Reddit attention and sentiment-intensity measures, paid-news attention and intensity, and
Google Trends search interest; these added blocks form the volatility chapter's HAR-X
ablation rungs, and Google Trends enters here only, not in the conditional-mean models.

\textbf{GARCH.} A GARCH(1,1) model \parencite{engle1982,bollerslev1986}, in which the
return is a constant mean plus a conditionally heteroscedastic shock,
\(\varepsilon_t = \sigma_t z_t\), whose variance follows
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha\,\varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta\,\sigma_{t-1}^2 .
\end{equation}
It is fit by maximum likelihood and re-estimated walk-forward---the parametric benchmark
for conditional variance.

\textbf{HAR-RV and HAR-X.} The heterogeneous autoregressive model of \textcite{corsi2009}
regresses next-week realised volatility on its own trailing averages over short, medium,
and long horizons; HAR-X augments that base with an exogenous block \(z_{t-1}\),
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{RV}_t =
    \underbrace{\beta_0
    + \beta_w\,\mathrm{RV}^{(w)}_{t-1}
    + \beta_m\,\mathrm{RV}^{(m)}_{t-1}
    + \beta_q\,\mathrm{RV}^{(q)}_{t-1}}_{\text{HAR-RV}}
    \; + \; \gamma'\, z_{t-1} \; + \; \varepsilon_t ,
\end{equation}
where \(\mathrm{RV}^{(w)}, \mathrm{RV}^{(m)}, \mathrm{RV}^{(q)}\) are the trailing 1-week,
4-week, and 12-week mean realised volatilities, and \(z_{t-1}\) is the added predictor
block---the six cross-asset realised volatilities, Reddit attention and intensity,
paid-news attention and intensity, or Google Trends, entered as separate ablation rungs
(\(z_{t-1}=\varnothing\) recovers HAR-RV). Both are estimated by OLS.

\textbf{Tree ensembles.} Random forest and XGBoost on the HAR-plus-cross-asset feature
set, isolating any nonlinear gain over the linear HAR-X.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol and significance tests}
\label{sec:method-eval}

All models are scored on the same 2023--2026 test window of \(n\) weeks; write \(r_t\) for the
realised return and \(\hat r_t\) for the forecast. Point-forecast accuracy is measured by
root-mean-squared and mean-absolute error,
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\tfrac1n \textstyle\sum_{t=1}^{n} (r_t - \hat r_t)^2}, \qquad
    \mathrm{MAE}  = \tfrac1n \textstyle\sum_{t=1}^{n} \lvert r_t - \hat r_t\rvert ,
\end{equation}
the primary metrics, since efficiency is a conditional-mean (magnitude) claim. The directional
lens is directional accuracy and its magnitude-weighted form,
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{DA} = \tfrac1n \textstyle\sum_{t=1}^{n}
        \mathbf{1}\!\left[\operatorname{sign} r_t = \operatorname{sign}\hat r_t\right],
    \qquad
    \mathrm{WDA} = \frac{\sum_{t} \lvert r_t\rvert\,
        \mathbf{1}\!\left[\operatorname{sign} r_t = \operatorname{sign}\hat r_t\right]}
        {\sum_{t} \lvert r_t\rvert} ,
\end{equation}
WDA weighting each correct call by the size of the move, so a missed large week costs more than
a missed flat one. Because the volatility target is non-negative, DA and WDA do not apply to it;
the volatility chapter instead scores direction with direction-of-change accuracy (DCA)---whether
the model correctly called the rise or fall in \(\log\mathrm{RV}\). Effect size relative to the
drift is the Campbell--Thompson out-of-sample \(R^2\) \parencite{campbell2008},
\begin{equation}
    R^2_{\text{OS}} = 1 - \frac{\sum_{t}(r_t - \hat r_t)^2}{\sum_{t}(r_t - \bar r_t)^2} ,
\end{equation}
with \(\bar r_t\) the prevailing-mean drift forecast, so \(R^2_{\text{OS}}>0\) means the model
beats the random walk out of sample.

Significance rests on the following tests. The \textbf{Diebold--Mariano} test of equal predictive
accuracy \parencite{diebold1995}, with a lag-1 Newey--West \parencite{newey1987} long-run
variance, compares each model to its
floor---the drift for returns, the na\"ive \(\mathrm{RV}_{t-1}\) random walk for
volatility. For returns it is reported under squared-error loss (the headline) and
absolute-error loss (a heavy-tail robustness check), and for volatility under the
QLIKE loss \parencite{patton2011}---robust, like squared error, to the noise in the
realised-volatility proxy, but far better powered than squared-error DM on heavy-tailed RV,
where a handful of extreme weeks swamp the loss differential. The \textbf{Pesaran--Timmermann} test
\parencite{pesaran1992} is the matching significance test for the directional (DA/WDA)
reading.

Two robustness layers accompany the headline tests: a breakdown by calendar sub-period
across 2023--2026, to confirm that performance is not driven by a single regime, and a
re-run of the full battery with the exceptional 2025 bull run excluded. The load-bearing
verdict throughout is the Diebold--Mariano test against the relevant floor---the drift for
returns, the na\"ive \(\mathrm{RV}_{t-1}\) random walk for volatility; the directional
results are a secondary, economically motivated lens.

\section{The Conditional Mean: Forecasting Weekly Returns}
\label{sec:mean}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This chapter evaluates the conditional-mean models of Section~\ref{sec:method-mean} on the
held-out 2023--2026 window, taking the weak- and semi-strong-form questions in turn. The
benchmark throughout is the prevailing-mean drift, and the load-bearing test is the
Diebold--Mariano (DM) comparison against it; the directional metrics are a secondary lens.
Table~\ref{tab:returns-leaderboard} collects the headline numbers.

\subsection{Weak form: own-history models}

At the series level, the own-history evidence already points to weak-form efficiency. A
Ljung--Box test finds only marginal linear autocorrelation in weekly returns
(\(p \approx 0.06\) at lag~10), and the generalised spectral test of \textcite{hong2003}
---which probes nonlinear as well as linear departures---\emph{fails to reject} the
martingale-difference null at every lag order tried (bootstrap \(p \approx 0.90\text{--}0.95\)
for \(p \in \{5,10,20\}\)). The conditional mean is therefore unpredictable from past returns
against both linear and nonlinear alternatives, while the same diagnostics strongly reject
independence in the \emph{squared} returns (\(p \approx 0\))---the predictable structure is
confined to the variance, not the mean.

The forecasting models confirm this. The AIC search over own-history ARIMA orders selects
\(\mathrm{ARIMA}(0,0,0)\) outright, so the best linear own-history forecast \emph{is} the
drift and there is nothing left to test. The only own-history variant that departs from it,
a 100-week rolling ARIMA, does not significantly beat it---a marginal, insignificant edge
that comes from tilting toward the 2025 trend and reverses once that year is removed. Past
returns, linear or nonlinear, carry no exploitable signal for the conditional mean.

\subsection{Semi-strong form: public information}

Expanding the information set does not change the verdict. Table~\ref{tab:returns-leaderboard}
reports each family's RMSE-best variant over the full ablation ladder---cross-asset returns,
the gold/silver ratio, monthly macro and the China PMI proxy, daily FRED indicators, COT
positioning, and Reddit, GDELT and paid-news sentiment---with the tree ensembles and the LSTM
free to combine them nonlinearly. The spread is strikingly tight: every family lands within
about one percentage point of the drift's 5.19\% weekly RMSE, and the best of them---an
XGBoost on the cross-asset returns plus paid-news sentiment---improves on the drift by just
0.05 points (\(R^2_{\text{OS}}=+1.8\%\)). Figure~\ref{fig:returns-rmse} shows the same picture:
the bars sit on the drift floor.

and both selection criteria---sixteen family-selections in total---not one beats the drift on

average a public-information model forecasts the weekly return no better than its own expanding
historical mean. The null hypothesis cannot be rejected for any model class---the linear ARIMAX,
VAR and MIDAS regressions, the tree ensembles, and the recurrent network all collapse onto the
drift. That model classes with such different inductive biases---a linear regression, a tree
ensemble, a recurrent network---converge on the same drift-equivalent forecast is a benign
instance of the model multiplicity \textcite{breiman2001} describes: when the conditional mean
carries no signal, the choice among well-fit model forms is immaterial. Every
feature in the ladder---cross-asset returns, positioning, macro, and sentiment alike---was
public information at the forecast date, so the whole ladder is one cumulative test of
semi-strong-form efficiency, and none of it beats the drift: the semi-strong null is not
rejected.

\subsection{Direction versus magnitude}

A weaker, direction-only signal does surface. Several families call the sign of the weekly
return more often than the drift does: because the prevailing mean of past returns is positive,
the drift always forecasts an up week, so its directional hit rate is merely the share of weeks
that rose. By the directionally-selected (WDA-best) criterion the linear models clear that
bar---DA \(\approx 0.62\) (Figure~\ref{fig:returns-wda})---and a
Pesaran--Timmermann (PT) test confirms genuine sign-timing in the linear ARIMAX and VAR
(\(p=0.004\) and \(0.005\)) and in the daily-input MIDAS. But the signal is magnitude-worthless.
The variants that maximise directional accuracy do so at the expense of point-forecast error:
they carry negative \(R^2_{\text{OS}}\) (VAR's WDA-best loses 14\% to the drift) and win no DM
test. It is also fragile---concentrated in the linear models and the post-hoc best-selected
variants, while across the full ablation ladders of the nonlinear families it appears at no
more than the chance rate (one of 38 random-forest variants, none of 38 for XGBoost). We read
it as consistent with the adaptive-markets view of \textcite{lo2004}---a weak, episodic signal,
not evidence against efficiency in the conditional mean.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/returns_wda.png}
    \caption{Directional accuracy of each family's WDA-best variant. The drift bar
    \emph{is} the always-up line (its sign is constant-positive); green beats it. Several

    \label{fig:returns-wda}
\end{figure}

That adaptive-markets reading is itself testable, and two structural checks bear it out. A rolling
52-week Pesaran--Timmermann test of an expanding-window directional forecast (built from the same
COT and paid-news signals) is insignificant pooled over the sample (\(p\approx0.15\)), yet clears
\(p<0.05\) in isolated 2021, 2022, 2024 and 2025 windows and is flat elsewhere---the signal
switches on and off rather than holding steady. A two-regime Markov-switching regression makes the
point structurally: it separates a long \emph{calm} regime (spells of \(\approx70\) weeks) from
short \emph{turbulent} bursts (\(\approx12\) weeks, six times the variance), and the paid-news
coefficient is an order of magnitude larger---and marginally significant
(\(p\approx0.08\))---inside the turbulent regime than the calm one
(Figure~\ref{fig:regime}). Directional predictability is therefore intermittent and regime-bound,
not constant: the adaptive-markets signature in structural form. The effect nonetheless stays
marginal and magnitude-worthless, so it sharpens the footnote without overturning the
efficiency verdict.

\subsection{Robustness}

The conclusion strengthens under scrutiny. Re-running the full battery with the exceptional
2025 bull removed---pooling 2023, 2024 and 2026, 122 weeks---again yields zero of sixteen
family-selections beating the floor; if anything the directionally-selected variants now fall
significantly \emph{behind} it (VAR, \(p=0.048\)). The per-period view
(Figure~\ref{fig:returns-period}) makes the point visually: no family holds a positive
out-of-sample \(R^2\) across all four sub-periods---a good year is offset by a bad one, the
signature of regime luck rather than skill, and the high directional accuracy of 2025 is
simply the bull run rewarding the always-up tilt. The expanding and 100-week rolling windows
tell the same story, with the rolling variants the worse of the two on balance. Weekly silver
returns are, on this evidence, unforecastable from public information.

\section{The Conditional Variance: Forecasting Weekly Volatility}
\label{sec:var}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Realised volatility is forecastable}

Where the conditional mean was unforecastable, the conditional variance is not. Every model
in the chapter---GARCH(1,1), the linear HAR-RV, and the two tree ensembles on the HAR
lags---beats the na\"ive \(\mathrm{RV}_{t-1}\) floor out of sample
(Table~\ref{tab:volatility-leaderboard}). HAR-RV cuts the na\"ive
mean-squared error by roughly a third (\(R^2_{\text{OS}}=0.29\) against the RV mean) and calls
the direction of the weekly volatility change in 71\% of test weeks; GARCH and the trees land
in the same band (\(R^2_{\text{OS}}=0.26\)--\(0.32\), DCA \(0.68\)--\(0.71\)), while the na\"ive
floor's \(R^2_{\text{OS}}\) is negative (and its direction-of-change accuracy zero by
construction). Figure~\ref{fig:volatility-fit} (Appendix~\ref{app:figures}) shows
the HAR forecast tracking the realised series across the full window---calm bands and run-ups
alike.

Significance depends on the loss. Under the proxy-robust QLIKE loss every model significantly
beats the na\"ive floor (Diebold--Mariano \(p<0.01\) throughout); under squared-error loss the
same comparisons are ties (\(p\approx 0.2\)--\(0.3\)). This is not a contradiction but a power
problem: realised volatility is heavy-tailed, so a handful of extreme weeks---chiefly the
late-January 2026 spike---dominate the squared-error differential and inflate its variance, which is why QLIKE
is the headline test (Section~\ref{sec:method-eval}). The consistent reading is that silver's
weekly volatility carries a strong, persistent, statistically detectable own-history
signal---in sharp contrast to its return.

\subsection{Cross-asset spillover and nonlinearity}

What does \emph{not} help is as informative as what does. Augmenting HAR-RV with the six
cross-asset realised volatilities---the volatility-spillover hypothesis---makes the linear
model significantly \emph{worse}: HAR+EXOG raises RMSE from 3.20 to 3.27 percent and loses to
bare HAR under both QLIKE and squared-error loss (\(p\approx 0.03\);
Table~\ref{tab:har-ablation}). At the weekly horizon the
cross-asset block adds estimation noise, not signal; silver's forecastable variance is its
own, not a spillover from gold, copper, or the broad risk complex.

Nonlinearity does not rescue it either. Random forest and XGBoost on the HAR lags sit in the
same RMSE band as linear HAR-RV, and the same trees on HAR+EXOG are no better than on HAR
alone (Diebold--Mariano ties)---the cross-asset block is dry for the trees too. What
\emph{does} matter is the multi-horizon structure itself: collapsing HAR to a single AR(1) lag
significantly degrades the random forest (\(p=0.001\)), confirming that the forecastable
signal is the short/medium/long volatility persistence Corsi's HAR is built to capture. The
parsimonious HAR is therefore the chapter's right benchmark, and the attention and sentiment
block of the next section is the only augmentation that earns its place.

\subsection{Sentiment and attention}
\label{sec:var-sentiment}
% HAR+Attention / sentiment ablation; small-but-significant effect; permutation importance.

The sentiment and attention ablation (Table~\ref{tab:har-ablation}) asks an incremental
question: does adding a public-information block improve on the same estimator (HAR, the random
forest, or XGBoost) using only its HAR realised-volatility lags? The answer is clearest for the linear HAR
specification. Reddit sentiment, combined Reddit
attention/sentiment, paid-news attention, and the combined paid-news rung all
significantly beat HAR-RV under QLIKE---and most under squared-error too---while Google Trends
and paid-news sentiment alone do not. Trends is the telling case: it posts the
\emph{lowest} RMSE of any linear rung yet ties bare HAR on QLIKE
(\(p\approx0.99\)), the clean RMSE-versus-QLIKE divergence the heavy-tailed loss predicts. The effect is therefore not a wholesale replacement of the
HAR volatility structure; it is a modest but statistically visible increment on top of
volatility persistence.

The tree-based results do not overturn that conclusion, but they make it more
conditional. Random forests record slightly better point errors for some augmented
specifications, yet the Diebold--Mariano tests mostly classify those gains as ties
against the RF(HAR) baseline. XGBoost gives the strongest nonlinear evidence:
the Reddit-attention rung significantly improves both MSE and QLIKE relative to
XGB(HAR), and several sentiment/attention variants improve one loss even when the
other remains a tie. Thus the public-information signal is useful for HAR and
selectively useful for XGBoost, but the evidence is not broad enough to claim that
tree ensembles dominate the HAR benchmark. Overall, HAR remains the central and
most stable benchmark, while sentiment and attention add small, loss-dependent
forecasting gains rather than changing the model ranking outright.

\subsection{Economic significance: a Value-at-Risk backtest}
\label{sec:var-backtest}

A QLIKE improvement is a statistical statement; a risk manager asks an economic one---does the
better volatility forecast produce better tail-risk numbers? We turn each weekly RV forecast
\(\hat\sigma_t\) into a one-week Value-at-Risk for the silver return,
\(\mathrm{VaR}_t(\alpha) = \mu + \hat\sigma_t\, q_\alpha\), with \(\mu\) the train-period drift and
\(q_\alpha\) the \(\alpha\)-quantile of the innovation (Normal headline, unit-variance
Student-\(t_5\) as a heavy-tail robustness check). A \emph{breach} is
\(r_t < \mathrm{VaR}_t(\alpha)\); we backtest unconditional coverage (Kupiec), the pinball loss (a
proper quantile score), and Expected Shortfall on the 174-week test window. Only \(\hat\sigma_t\)
differs across rows (Table~\ref{tab:var-backtest}), so any calibration gap is attributable to the
volatility forecast itself.

breaches, \(p=0.003\)). Treating last week's volatility as this week's risk produces an unusable

lives in the deep tail}. At 1\% even bare HAR is borderline (5 breaches, \(p=0.043\)), and
HAR\,+\,Reddit attention---the chapter's best parsimonious extension---is the only rung to restore

variance is forecastable, and the backtest shows the forecastable moment is the one a risk system

attention---is materially better calibrated than the random-walk benchmark a manager would
otherwise default to.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The two results chapters are not two studies but one decomposition seen in full. The
conditional-moment factorisation of Section~\ref{sec:framework} splits the weekly return into a
predictable mean and a time-varying variance, \(r_t = \mu_t + \sigma_t z_t\), and the thesis
asked whether each moment is forecastable from its own past and public information under one
benchmark protocol. The verdict is sharp and opposite for the two. The conditional mean
(Section~\ref{sec:mean}) is not forecastable: the own-history null survives---the AIC search
selects the drift itself and the generalised spectral test fails to reject the
martingale-difference property---and so does the semi-strong null, with no model in the eight
family ladder beating the drift floor out of sample. The conditional variance
(Section~\ref{sec:var}) plainly is forecastable: HAR, GARCH and the tree ensembles all clear the
na\"ive realised-volatility floor by a third of its mean-squared error. ``The mean is a
martingale, the variance is not'' is therefore a single empirical statement about one series---not a failure on the mean
redeemed by a success on the variance.

That pairing is fully consistent with market efficiency rather than in tension with it. The
efficient-market hypothesis \parencite{fama1970,fama1991} constrains the conditional mean---the
risk-adjusted expected return---and says nothing about the conditional variance. Forecastable
volatility is not a tradable arbitrage: knowing that next week is likely to be turbulent does not
tell an investor which way the price will move, and an efficient market processing information in
bursts should in fact display exactly the volatility clustering that HAR exploits. The variance
result thus strengthens rather than undercuts the efficiency reading: the one moment that carries
genuine, persistent structure is precisely the one efficiency leaves unconstrained, and the
moment efficiency does constrain is the one we cannot forecast.

The only crack in the mean's efficiency is directional, weak, and best read through an
adaptive-markets lens. A sign-timing signal surfaces in the linear models---Pesaran--Timmermann
significance from COT positioning and the paid-news title score in ARIMAX and VAR---and the
100-week rolling ARIMA shows a transient edge over the expanding drift; but both are
magnitude-worthless (they win no Diebold--Mariano test and carry negative out-of-sample
\(R^2\)), concentrated in the 2025 bull, and non-replicating across the nonlinear families.
Predictability that is
episodic, regime-bound, and unexploitable is the signature the adaptive-markets hypothesis of
\textcite{lo2004} anticipates: efficiency as a time-varying equilibrium that tightens and
loosens with conditions, not an absolute law. The regime analysis of Section~\ref{sec:mean}
puts this beyond interpretation---a rolling directional test that is significant only in
isolated windows, and a Markov-switching model in which the predictive coefficient concentrates
in short turbulent regimes (Figure~\ref{fig:regime}). We therefore read these directional
traces---and the rolling window's fleeting advantage---as a caveat consistent with adaptive
efficiency, not as evidence against it.

The sentiment evidence sharpens the same mean-versus-variance contrast and illustrates the gap
between statistical and economic significance. Tested as an explicit ablation on both moments, 
public attention and sentiment never \emph{significantly} improved the mean---no rung beat the drift on a
Diebold--Mariano test---even though some sentiment feed entered six of the eight families'
RMSE-best variants; that recurring appearance stays within noise, so it reinforces rather than
dents the efficiency verdict. For the variance the verdict flips. Measured against the HAR
baseline (each model judged against itself on HAR lags only), Reddit and paid-news attention cut
the linear HAR's QLIKE loss at \(p<0.01\)---under the proxy-robust loss that is the chapter's
primary metric for heavy-tailed volatility---a robust improvement consistent with the
media-sentiment effects of \textcite{tetlock2007} and the sentiment-augmented volatility
forecasts of \textcite{audrino2020}. The gain is small in magnitude---it sharpens an
already-strong HAR rather than transforming it---but statistically solid under the loss that
matters, and it appears only in the second moment. That asymmetry is itself a finding: public
attention co-moves with risk, not with direction.

Read together, the chapters carry a practical asymmetry for anyone trading silver: the level and
direction of next week's return are not forecastable from public information, but its risk is.
That is exactly the input a mean-variance or value-at-risk framework consumes---position sizing,
option pricing, and tail-risk budgeting all run on the conditional variance, not the conditional
mean. The mean-variance framing of the title is in that sense not decorative: the forecastable
moment is the one risk management actually uses.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Weekly silver returns are indistinguishable from a martingale difference under magnitude
loss, consistent with weak- and semi-strong-form efficiency in the economically relevant
sense; the residual directional dependence is statistically detectable but unexploitable,
which efficiency theory permits; and its episodic, regime-bound character is consistent
with---though not proof of---the adaptive-markets view \parencite{lo2004} in which such
efficiency is the equilibrium outcome rather than the assumption. One story, three layers,
no tension.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
%  Contributions (relocated from the Introduction). Fold into the conclusion prose
%  when it is written, or keep as a short standalone paragraph.
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\textbf{Contributions.} The thesis makes four contributions. First, it evaluates a full
model ladder---classical, machine-learning, and deep-learning---for \emph{both} moments
of weekly silver returns under one drift benchmark and one significance battery, so that
model families are compared on a level footing rather than across incommensurable
setups. Second, it makes the conditional-mean / conditional-variance factorisation
explicit, turning what could read as ``returns failed, volatility worked'' into a single
coherent efficiency statement. Third, it builds a domain-appropriate sentiment
pipeline---Twitter-RoBERTa for Reddit, FinBERT for financial news---and treats sentiment
as a falsifiable ablation on both moments rather than assuming it helps. Fourth, it
documents a carefully qualified set of negative results on semi-strong predictability of
the mean, separating magnitude from direction and showing where an apparent directional
signal fails to replicate across model classes.

\textbf{Limitations.} Several caveats bound these conclusions. First, the evidence is one
asset over one decade (591 weeks, 2015--2026) with a 174-week out-of-sample window that the
exceptional 2025 bull dominates; a failure to reject efficiency is not proof of it, and the
power to detect a small predictable component in a heavy-tailed weekly return is limited.
Second, sentiment and attention remain model-scored proxies drawn from a bounded set of
channels---reputable English news (paid, full-text), multilingual GDELT headlines, Reddit, and
Google-Trends search---so an effect carried by sources or platforms outside this set, or one a
sharper measure of tone would catch, could be missed. Third, the entire study is at the weekly
horizon: efficiency could bind less tightly, or predictability surface, intraday or monthly,
and the daily-input siblings only partly probe this while still targeting the weekly return.
Fourth, realised volatility is built from daily squared returns rather than intraday data---a
coarse proxy for the true integrated variance.

\textbf{Future work.} Each limitation points to an extension. Higher-frequency (five-minute)
realised variance would sharpen the volatility target and let the mean be re-tested intraday,
where microstructure may admit fleeting predictability the weekly view averages away. The
regime-switching test of the directional signal (Section~\ref{sec:mean}) and the VaR backtest of
the volatility forecasts (Section~\ref{sec:var-backtest}) are deliberately first passes: a richer
multi-state or smoothly time-varying efficiency model, and a cost-aware volatility-managed strategy
backtested net of transaction costs, would push each further and turn the tail-risk result into a
full economic-value study. Finally, applying the same two-moment framework to other metals---gold,
platinum, copper---would show whether the weekly efficiency found here is specific to silver or a
shared feature of liquid metal markets.

\subsection{Sources}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Market prices} --- Yahoo Finance, daily. The three metals are global
    benchmarks; the equity, dollar, volatility, and oil series are US-centric:
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item silver (\texttt{SI=F}): COMEX front-month futures, the global silver benchmark --- the forecast target
        \item gold (\texttt{GC=F}): silver's closest monetary substitute and dominant co-mover
        \item copper (\texttt{HG=F}): industrial-demand proxy (about half of silver demand is industrial)
        \item US dollar index (\texttt{DX-Y.NYB}): the dollar against a basket of major currencies; metals are dollar-priced, so a stronger dollar weighs on silver
        \item S\&P~500 (\texttt{\textasciicircum GSPC}): US large-cap equities --- broad risk appetite
        \item VIX (\texttt{\textasciicircum VIX}): 30-day implied volatility from S\&P~500 options --- the market's fear / risk-off gauge
        \item oil (\texttt{CL=F}): WTI crude, the US benchmark --- commodity-complex and inflation proxy
    \end{itemize}
    The six cross-asset weekly log-returns form the EXOG set \parencite{batten2010,zhang2010}.

    \item \textbf{Rates and inflation} --- FRED, US, daily:
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item 10-year TIPS real yield (\texttt{DFII10}): the inflation-adjusted return on
        10-year US Treasury bonds---the ``real'' interest rate. Silver pays no income, so a
        higher real yield makes interest-bearing bonds the more attractive store of value
        and tends to pull the metal down.
        \item 10-year breakeven inflation (\texttt{T10YIE}): the average inflation rate
        markets expect over the next decade (the gap between ordinary and
        inflation-protected 10-year Treasury yields). Silver is held as an inflation hedge,
        so rising expectations can support it.
        \item initial jobless claims (\texttt{ICSA}): the number of new US
        unemployment-benefit filings each week---a timely read on the labour market and the
        economy's direction, with rising claims signalling a weakening economy.
    \end{itemize}
    Each enters as a weekly change.

    \item \textbf{Macroeconomic} --- FRED, US, monthly:
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item Consumer Price Index (\texttt{CPIAUCSL}): the headline measure of realised US
        consumer-price inflation
        \item federal funds rate (\texttt{FEDFUNDS}): the Fed's overnight policy interest
        rate, which drives real yields and the dollar
        \item industrial production (\texttt{INDPRO}): the Fed's index of US factory, mining,
        and utility output; about half of silver demand is industrial, so stronger output
        lifts physical demand for the metal
        \item M2 money supply (\texttt{M2SL}): a broad measure of money in circulation (cash
        plus bank deposits and near-money); rapid growth signals future inflation, which
        tends to lift hard assets like silver
    \end{itemize}
    These enter under a point-in-time availability rule rather than a naive shift: a value
    is visible only once it would actually have been released, with three publication lags
    kept per series.

    \item \textbf{Positioning} --- CFTC (US) Commitments of Traders, COMEX silver, weekly:
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item managed-money net positioning: speculative (fund) sentiment
        \item commercial net positioning: hedger and producer positioning
    \end{itemize}
    Each as a share of open interest.

    \item \textbf{Business sentiment (PMI)} --- FRED, monthly: a China business-confidence
    proxy (\texttt{CHNBSCICP02STSAM}), a balance-of-opinion level standing in for industrial
    demand from silver's largest physical consumer. Unlike the macro block (three pre-lagged
    publication lags), it enters as a single point-in-time level---the most recent value
    released by its publication date (a 31-day reporting lag)---taken as the week's last
    observation and lagged one week by each model, exactly like the gold/silver ratio. It is
    kept as its own ablation group rather than folded into the US macro set.

    \item \textbf{Sentiment} --- Reddit, GDELT, NewsAPI.ai, Google Trends (English-language / worldwide):
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item Reddit posts (r/WallStreetSilver, r/Silverbugs; via Arctic Shift): retail attention and tone, scored with Twitter-RoBERTa \parencite{loureiro2022}
        \item GDELT news headlines: global media tone, scored with FinBERT \parencite{araci2019}
        \item NewsAPI.ai (Event Registry) paid news: reputable English-language sources, from 2015 and at least one article per week, scored with FinBERT \parencite{araci2019} on the article title
        \item Google Trends ``silver'' search interest: worldwide retail attention (volatility chapter only)
    \end{itemize}
    Each text source is averaged to a weekly mean. GDELT is sparse---it begins only in late
    2017 and has zero-article weeks even inside the test window---so the news series is a
    documented limitation; the paid NewsAPI.ai feed covers the full sample and is the
    cleaner news source, while the volatility chapter (Section~\ref{sec:var}) draws on
    Reddit, paid news, and Google Trends instead. From each text feed that chapter derives
    the weekly attention and tone-intensity features of Section~\ref{sec:data-sentiment},
    plus a within-week tone-dispersion measure (the standard deviation of daily tone)
    for Reddit and paid news; dispersion is dropped from every ablation rung after
    exploratory analysis found no correlation with realised volatility.

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Technical-indicator formulas}
\label{app:tech}

Every indicator in the TECH group (Section~\ref{sec:data}) is a transform of the weekly
(W-FRI) silver close \(P_t\), with \(r_t=\log(P_t/P_{t-1})\) the weekly log-return. Write
\(\mathrm{EMA}_k\) and \(\mathrm{SMA}_k\) for the exponential and simple moving averages of
span/window \(k\) weeks, \(\sigma_k\) for the rolling \(k\)-week standard deviation, and
\(\min_k,\max_k\) for the rolling \(k\)-week low and high; RSI uses exponentially-smoothed
average gains and losses. Each is built raw and lagged one week before use.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \small
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.6}
    \caption{Technical-indicator definitions (weekly silver close \(P_t\)).}
    \label{tab:tech}
    \begin{tabular}{l l c}
        \toprule
        Indicator & Definition & Window (wk) \\
        \midrule
        MACD line         & \(\mathrm{EMA}_{12}(P_t)-\mathrm{EMA}_{26}(P_t)\) & 12,\,26 \\
        MACD histogram    & \(\mathrm{MACD}_t-\mathrm{EMA}_{9}(\mathrm{MACD}_t)\) & 9 \\
        RSI               & \(100-\dfrac{100}{1+\mathrm{RS}_t},\ \ \mathrm{RS}_t=\dfrac{\mathrm{EMA}_{14}(\text{gains})}{\mathrm{EMA}_{14}(\text{losses})}\) & 14 \\
        Momentum          & \(\textstyle\sum_{i=0}^{4} r_{t-i}\) & 5 \\
        Rate of change    & \(P_t/P_{t-k}-1,\ \ k\in\{13,26,52\}\) & 13,\,26,\,52 \\
        Price-to-MA       & \(P_t/\mathrm{SMA}_{13}(P_t)-1\) & 13 \\
        Donchian position & \(\dfrac{2\,(P_t-\min_{52})}{\max_{52}-\min_{52}}-1\) & 52 \\
        Bollinger \%B     & \(\dfrac{P_t-(\mathrm{SMA}_{20}-2\,\sigma_{20})}{4\,\sigma_{20}}\) & 20 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}
